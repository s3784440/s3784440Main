    ALTITUDE

    ALTITUDE is a proposed mobile software solution which aims to assist pilots while flying aircraft in the skies above. In the present day, the use of mobile technologies such as tablets has become common practice in schools and at home in terms of their applications and usage. The flight deck of an aircraft whilst above the ground is an extremely busy environment, where the inputs and decision making of the pilots ultimately dictate the outcome of the plane. Aviation is a highly dynamic industry in which the culture and technology are constantly ever-changing, and this makes the project a perfect opportunity to be implemented for the emphasis on today’s modern era.

    The workload of pilots during take-off climb and coming to approach are at its highest because they will have to perform multiple checklists to configure the aircraft at each stage. Aviators are highly-trained professionals who have the responsibility for the aircraft they are in command for and getting it from Point A to Point B safely. Modern aviation is filled with automation and sophistication in technology, however, the pilot has to always to be alert for maintaining safe operation of the aircraft at all times. Software solution of a similar kind have already existed; however, it is seen that it is still an opportunity to improve and upgrade current solutions. Simpler solutions and ease of use consume less time for pilots to learn and use while they are flying.

    The program is designed to be simple and intuitive; it is projected to have three different modes in which the pilots can change seamlessly.

    These modes are Ground, Departure, Flight, and Approach:

    Ground Mode is designed to assist pilots in the sense of navigating around an airport and provide relevant information such as briefing on notices and memos. The ground environment is a busy space at airports may have dozens of aircraft moving simultaneously. For the pilots, it is their responsibility to avoid any potential hazards or obstacles which may impact the aircraft’s operation. The system would primarily assist in plotting taxiway routes and also the flight requirements such as fuel quantity, take-off weight, temperature factor, Air Traffic frequency and center of gravity calculations (retrospect to the aircraft type).

    Departure Mode is designed to be used from the Runway up to when it has left the airfield’s airspace, providing information such as speed values and waypoint tracks. The speed values will be based on the inputs made in Ground Mode. The critical speeds to be cross-checked with the onboard flight computer, to minimise the risk of errors and discrepancies. The crew would have to plot their course through the use of waypoints; these are GPS coordinate positions which serves as markers for aircraft traffic. The software solution would serve as a secondary backup system during flight, in case the primary Flight Management Computer FMS become redundant or experience failure.

    Flight Mode is designed to be used during a climb to cruising altitude or above 10,000 feet. The workload of the pilots after climbing above this level is significantly reduced, however, they will still need to be alert of the situation in case of any problems arising in this stage. The system would provide information on possible Alternate Airports in case there is a requirement to divert. During this phase, the crew would be more relaxed than any other stages of flight. There is a term called ‘controlled-rest’ where one pilot would take rest while the other would be flying the plane and monitoring the system. The risk of controlled-rest is that in case both pilots are simultaneously ‘resting’, there would be no-one monitoring the aircraft’s systems. An Aural Warning feature would automatically be enabled in the software solution, providing 15-minute intervals of silence and 30 seconds prior to the system would prompt have inputs made from the pilots. A high- and low-pitched sound will play otherwise if no inputs have been made. This is designed to alert the pilots to make sure they are aware and well informed of their situation.

    Approach mode is designed to help assist with the approach and landing of the aircraft; the system in this stage would strictly give only the relevant information as it will be the most critical stage of flight. The pilots can use the system to open charts, input taxiway routing, and ATC frequency information. The system will be almost ‘static’ yet still interactive so that the pilots can focus on managing and configuring the aircraft for landing. Upon landing and the aircraft is safely on the taxiway, the system would prompt to switch to Ground Mode with the information from the Approach Mode entries.

    The philosophy of ALTITUDE is a modular system in which features of the program can be upgraded and improved. The pilots can also make their change the layout of the modules as to their preference.

    As the software is designed to be a mobile solution, this means that it has a variety of programming languages which are compatible and can be executed almost on any device. The language of preference for the test bed of the solution would be Java because it can run on Android or iOS. It is more accessible and consumes less time to build a solution than using other exotic programming languages. As the solution will operate independently of the aircraft’s computer systems, the Civil Aviation Safety Authority permits devices with a minimum size of a tablet to serve as a solution.

    The skills required to make the program is to have a developer of more than 5 years’ experience because of the nature and magnitude of the intended purpose of the solution. This is to prevent minor errors which could cascade and cause system failures. A developer with Aviation Knowledge would be beneficial when it comes to understanding the philosophy and discipline of management in the flight-deck. It is important that both parties – user and developer – consult with each other for the interface and functions of the final product version.

    The outcome from the project, if it becomes successful, is that initially, it would be used for General Aviation pilots for their personal use and training. Future versions of the system would be designed to mimic the systems already on-board larger aircraft so that training pilots are able to get an insight on how to manage automation and flying. The solution may hold a firm recognition in the industry and when other companies want to further develop it, they will require licensing from the original developer. The main mission of the solution is simple; to improve flight safety and management.
